A tree is a graph that is connected and acyclic, and a star is a tree with a center that is connected to all vertices.

So, now we want to prove that a tree is not a star if and only if its complement graph is connected.

for the second implication we will proceed using contraposition.

\( T \) is a star \( \Longrightarrow \overline{T} \) is not connected.

We suppose that \( T \) is a star, so the center is isolated in \( \overline{T} \), therefore \( \overline{T} \) is not connected.

and for the first implication there are two methods, the first one is quite automatic, and presents the way we proceed generally when we try to prove connectedness.

so we suppose that \( T \) is not a star:
and let \( x, y \in V(T) \):
if \( (x, y) \notin E(T) \), then \( (x, y) \in E(\overline{T}) \)
if \( (x, y) \in E(T) \), we have two cases:

if \( x \) is a leaf, using the hypothesis that our graph is not a star, there exists a vertex \( z \) that is not connected to \( y \), so in \( \overline{T} \), \( x \) and \( y \) are connected by \( z \).

if \( x \) and \( y \) are not leafs, then there exist \( z \) connected to \( x \), and \( t \) connected to \( y \), and as \( T \) is a tree, its vertices have not the same neighbours, so in \( \overline{T}, \) we have the chain : \( x - t - z - y \).

So the complement graph is connected.

Yet, we can just say: as \( T \) is a tree, there exists a leaf \( f \), and \( f \) is connected to all vertices except one, but this one is connected to another vertex, which is connected to \( f \).

\( \square \)
