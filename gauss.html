Here, we will calculate Gauss integral: \[ G = \int_0^{+\infty} e^{-t^{2}} dt \] And for this we gonna make it more complicated and easier :) \[ I = \int_{\mathbf{R}^2_+} e^{-(x^{2}+y^{2})} d(x,y) \] Using Fubini theorem, we get: \[ I = \int_{\mathbf{R}_+} \int_{\mathbf{R}_+} e^{-(x^{2}+y^{2})} dx dy \] \[ I = \int_{\mathbf{R}_+} e^{-x^{2}} dx \times \int_{\mathbf{R}_+} e^{-y^{2}}dy \] And yes: \[ I = G^2 \] And by using the coordinates \( (\rho, \theta) \) \[ x = \rho cos \theta \] \[ y = \rho sin \theta \] And we have the determinant of the jacobian matrix: \[ |J| = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial \rho} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta}\\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial \rho} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} cos\theta & -\rho sin\theta\\ sin\theta & \rho cos\theta \end{pmatrix} = \rho \] Therefore: \[ I = \int_0^{\infty} \int_0^{\pi/2} \rho e^{-\rho^2}d \rho d \theta \] \[ = \frac{\pi}{2} \int_0^{\infty} \rho e^{-\rho^2}d \rho \] \[ = \frac{\pi}{2} \frac{1}{2} [ e^{-\rho^2}]_0^{\infty} = \frac{\pi}{4} \]

And hence, we have our integral: \[ G = \sqrt{I} = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \]
